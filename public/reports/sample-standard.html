Step 3: Conviction — 3.8 / 10

Conviction is the degree to which you fully commit to a shot before executing it. A high score means you rarely second-guess yourself mid-swing. A low score indicates hesitation, doubt, or partial commitment — which directly affects both accuracy and distance.

What your score means

At 3.8, your conviction is notably below the average for your handicap range (5.9). This suggests you frequently step into shots without full mental commitment. The most common pattern: you've selected the right club and target, but at the top of the backswing, a voice says "maybe I should have..." — and the shot is already compromised.

Key pattern detected: Your conviction drops most significantly on approach shots to par 4s (holes 4–9), suggesting early-round pressure erodes your commitment before you've settled into the round.

Recommendations

1. Implement a commitment checkpoint — before every shot, ask yourself: "Am I 100% committed to this shot?" If the answer is not an immediate yes, step away and restart your pre-shot routine.

2. Use the Skills Developer — the "Conviction Under Pressure" report (15 credits) contains exercises specifically designed for your pattern.

Step 6: Acceptance — 3.2 / 10

Acceptance measures how effectively you process the outcome of each shot — good or bad — and release it before moving to the next. This is the most commonly underdeveloped step, and also the one with the greatest impact on enjoyment.

What your score means

At 3.2, you are carrying unprocessed emotion from bad shots into subsequent holes. Your data shows a clear pattern: after a bogey or worse, your scores on the next two holes average +0.8 strokes above your baseline. This "emotional carry-over" is one of the most trainable patterns in golf psychology.

Key pattern detected: Your acceptance score drops further when playing competitively vs. socially, suggesting external pressure amplifies your inability to release bad outcomes.

Recommendations

1. The 10-step walk — give yourself exactly 10 steps after a bad shot to feel whatever you feel. At step 10, consciously leave it behind.

2. Use the Skills Developer — the "Post-Shot Release" report targets this exact pattern.
